Hi there! I'm Jacob Myers, a cybersecurity student at UT Austin. This blog/showcase is an example of one of our first projects. On this page, I'll discuss some of the methods surrounding the creation of the site, as well as some cybersecurity related news I feel is important for others to become familiar with.

Afterward, the HTML configurations were manually configured to personalize the page and create the blog below. Could Chat GPT Become a Security Threat?

Phishing, ChatGPT, AI

ChatGPT, or Chat Generative Pretrained Transformer, is a new language processing artificial intelligence that can generate responses that sounds eerily human-like. ChatGPT is a transformative breakthrough for many. Marketing firms are able to use the AI to get the ball rolling on advertisements. If needed, the AI can even produce code for development, scripting, and much more. Or, if desired, it can discuss philosophical concepts. Overall, this tool can increase productivity exponentially if used properly.

However, with ChatGPT's incredible intelligence, it could potentially pose a security-related threat in the near future. For instance, bad actors could use ChatGPT's language skills to develop sophisticated, human-like phishing emails. It could become difficult for people in the workforce to differentiate, as previous indicators of phishing may be elimated with the introduction of ChatGPT. It's worth noting that I used ChatGPT to brainstorm for this very blog!

LastPass Notifies User of Breach

Encryption, Password Management

On December 22nd of 2022, the well-known password manager, LastPass, notified users of a breach resulting in the intrusion of archived backup storage. The threat actor was able to obtain a cloud storage access key as well as a dual storage container decryption key. As a result of this breach, the threat actor was able to copy information from the backup that contained basic customer information such as customer names, names of end-users, billing addresses, email addresses, phone numbers, and IP addresses associated with the end-user.

Most importantly, the threat actor was able to copy data from the backup customer data vault. It's important to note that the data from this vault contained unencrypted links to website URLs associated with passwords. However, the passwords and usernames associated with the URLs are encrypted via 256-bit AES encryption. This breach reaches as far back as August of 2022 when LastPass claimed its front-end was compromised. It's become quite clear that this breach has gone much farther than the company has intended. Personally, once informed of this breach, have changed password managers.
